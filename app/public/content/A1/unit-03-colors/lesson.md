# Colors & Descriptions

Describing the world around you is one of the most satisfying early milestones in Spanish. Colors are essential — you'll use them shopping, giving directions, and describing people and places.

> In Mexico, **café** is used for brown — not *marrón*, which is more common in Spain.

## Adjective Agreement

In Spanish, adjectives must agree in **gender** and **number** with the noun they describe. This means the color word changes depending on what it describes.

**Masculine singular → add nothing (or -o):**
- El carro **rojo** (the red car)
- El libro **azul** (the blue book)

**Feminine singular → change -o to -a:**
- La manzana **roja** (the red apple)
- La casa **blanca** (the white house)

**Plural → add -s (or -es after a consonant):**
- Los carros **rojos** (the red cars)
- Las casas **blancas** (the white houses)

Colors ending in **-e** or a consonant (like *verde*, *azul*, *gris*) don't change for gender — only for plural:
- El libro **verde** / La manzana **verde**
- Los libros **verdes** / Las manzanas **verdes**

## Describing Things

> — ¿De qué color es tu carro? *(What color is your car?)*
> — Mi carro es gris. *(My car is gray.)*

> — ¿Cómo es la casa? *(What is the house like?)*
> — Es grande y blanca. *(It's big and white.)*

## 🇲🇽 Cultural Note

Color plays a big role in Mexican culture. The colors of the Mexican flag — **verde, blanco, rojo** — appear everywhere. Markets (*mercados*) are famous for their vibrant use of color in textiles, ceramics, and flowers. When describing something as brown, Mexicans say **café** (literally "coffee") — the same word used for the drink.
