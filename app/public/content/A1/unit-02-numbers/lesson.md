# Numbers & Counting

Numbers are one of the first things you'll use in real conversations — telling someone your age, asking for prices at a market, or giving your phone number. In Mexican Spanish, numbers follow a simple pattern once you learn the building blocks.

## The Tens: 20–100

| Number | Spanish |
|--------|---------|
| 20 | veinte |
| 30 | treinta |
| 40 | cuarenta |
| 50 | cincuenta |
| 60 | sesenta |
| 70 | setenta |
| 80 | ochenta |
| 90 | noventa |
| 100 | cien |

For numbers between the tens, use **y** (and):

> **Treinta y cinco** = 35
> **Cuarenta y dos** = 42
> **Setenta y ocho** = 78

## Asking About Prices

In Mexican markets and shops, you'll often hear prices spoken aloud. The key phrase is **¿Cuánto cuesta?** (How much does it cost?).

> — ¿Cuánto cuesta? *(How much does it cost?)*
> — Cuesta cincuenta pesos. *(It costs fifty pesos.)*

> — ¿Cuántos años tienes? *(How old are you? — informal)*
> — Tengo veinticinco años. *(I'm twenty-five years old.)*

Note that **uno** becomes **un** before a masculine noun and **una** before a feminine noun:
- **un libro** (one book)
- **una manzana** (one apple)

## Phone Numbers

Mexicans typically group phone numbers in pairs when saying them aloud. A number like 55-12-34-56 would be said:

> **Cincuenta y cinco, doce, treinta y cuatro, cincuenta y seis.**

## 🇲🇽 Cultural Note

In Mexican markets (*mercados*), bargaining is common and numbers fly fast. Vendors may quote prices quickly — listening for the tens and units separately helps. Also note that in Mexico, **cien** is used for exactly 100, but **ciento** is used for 101 and above (*ciento uno*, *ciento veinte*, etc.).
