# Mexican History & Politics

Understanding Mexican history is inseparable from understanding how Mexicans speak about identity, power, and justice. The vocabulary and concepts of this unit appear constantly in newspapers, political speech, and everyday conversation.

## Key Historical Periods

Mexican history is structured around a set of defining ruptures and transformations. Every educated Mexican knows these periods by name.

| Period | Dates | Key concept |
|---|---|---|
| *La Conquista* | 1519–1521 | Spanish conquest of the Aztec empire |
| *La Colonia* | 1521–1821 | Three centuries of colonial rule |
| *La Independencia* | 1810–1821 | War of Independence from Spain |
| *La Reforma* | 1855–1876 | Liberal reform; separation of church and state |
| *El Porfiriato* | 1876–1911 | Dictatorship of Porfirio Díaz |
| *La Revolución* | 1910–1920 | Mexican Revolution |
| *El período del PRI* | 1929–2000 | Single-party rule under the PRI |
| *La alternancia* | 2000–present | Democratic rotation of power |

## Essential Political Vocabulary

> *el estado* — the state (government entity)
> *el gobierno* — the government (the administration in power)
> *la soberanía* — sovereignty
> *la ciudadanía* — citizenship / citizenry
> *el sufragio* — suffrage / the vote
> *el mandato* — term in office
> *la corrupción* — corruption
> *la impunidad* — impunity
> *la rendición de cuentas* — accountability
> *el Estado de derecho* — rule of law

The distinction between *el estado* and *el gobierno* is important: *el estado* is the permanent institutional structure; *el gobierno* is the transient political leadership.

## Key Figures and Their Legacy

**Benito Juárez** (1806–1872) is arguably the most revered figure in Mexican political history — a Zapotec Indigenous man who became president and defended the republic against foreign intervention. His phrase *"El respeto al derecho ajeno es la paz"* (Respect for the rights of others is peace) remains part of the national consciousness.

**Emiliano Zapata** (1879–1919) led the agrarian wing of the Revolution under the slogan *"Tierra y Libertad"* (Land and Liberty). His image is still invoked in contemporary social movements.

**Lázaro Cárdenas** (1895–1970) nationalized the oil industry in 1938 (*la expropiación petrolera*), a defining act of economic sovereignty still celebrated on March 18.

## The PRI and Democratic Transition

For 71 years, Mexico was governed by a single party — the **Partido Revolucionario Institucional (PRI)**. The system was characterized by:

- *El corporativismo* — incorporation of unions, farmers, and professionals into the party structure
- *El presidencialismo* — enormous concentration of power in the executive
- *El dedazo* — the incumbent president's tradition of personally designating his successor

The democratic transition (*la alternancia*) began in 2000 when Vicente Fox of the PAN won the presidency, ending PRI dominance.

## Contemporary Political Discourse

Modern political conversation in Mexico revolves around several persistent themes:

- *La seguridad pública* — public safety, in the context of organized crime
- *La corrupción e impunidad* — corruption and the failure to prosecute it
- *Los derechos humanos* — human rights
- *La desigualdad* — inequality (economic and social)
- *La autonomía indígena* — Indigenous autonomy and rights

These terms appear daily in newspapers and political speech. Recognizing them unlocks access to Mexican civic life.

## 🇲🇽 Cultural Note: El 18 de Marzo y el Petróleo

On March 18, 1938, President Lázaro Cárdenas nationalized Mexico's oil industry, expropriating assets from foreign companies. The day is still a national holiday (*el Día de la Expropiación Petrolera*). The state oil company *PEMEX* (Petróleos Mexicanos) became a symbol of national sovereignty. For many Mexicans, the oil nationalization represents one of the most complete expressions of the Revolution's promise — that Mexico's natural wealth belongs to its people. The political vocabulary around *soberanía energética* (energy sovereignty) continues to shape debates about PEMEX to this day.
