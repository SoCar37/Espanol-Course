# Unit 3: If-Clauses (Si Clauses)

## Conditional Sentences — Real, Hypothetical, and Impossible

Si clauses are the engine of hypothetical thinking in Spanish. Whether you are talking about likely events, imaginary scenarios, or things that never happened, the right combination of tenses makes your meaning clear. This unit covers all three types.

---

## Type 1 — Real / Likely Conditions

Use this when the condition is **real, habitual, or likely to happen**. There is no subjunctive involved.

**Formula: si + present indicative + present/future/command**

> *Si **tienes** tiempo, **llámame**.*
> If you have time, call me.

> *Si **llueve**, no voy a salir.*
> If it rains, I'm not going out.

> *Si **comes** bien, **te sentirás** mejor.*
> If you eat well, you'll feel better.

This is for real-world if-then statements — things that genuinely could happen.

---

## Type 2 — Hypothetical / Contrary to Present Fact

Use this when the condition is **imaginary, unlikely, or contrary to current reality**. This is where the past subjunctive enters.

**Formula: si + past subjunctive + conditional**

> *Si **tuviera** más dinero, **viajaría** por todo México.*
> If I had more money (but I don't), I would travel all over Mexico.

> *Si **fuera** presidente, **cambiaría** muchas cosas.*
> If I were president (but I'm not), I would change many things.

> *Si **pudiera** vivir en otra época, **elegiría** los años 70.*
> If I could live in another era, I would choose the 70s.

> *¿Qué **harías** si te **tocaras** la lotería?*
> What would you do if you won the lottery?

The key signal: the condition is **not true right now**. The past subjunctive in the si-clause and the conditional in the main clause work as a matched pair.

---

## Type 3 — Contrary to Past Fact

Use this for conditions that **didn't happen** — imagining an alternative past.

**Formula: si + past perfect subjunctive + conditional perfect**

First, meet the **past perfect subjunctive**: it is formed with **hubiera/hubieras/hubiera** + past participle.

| Person | Past perfect subjunctive |
|--------|-------------------------|
| yo | hubiera hablado |
| tú | hubieras comido |
| él/ella | hubiera vivido |
| nosotros | hubiéramos llegado |
| ustedes | hubieran sabido |

> *Si **hubiera estudiado** más, **habría pasado** el examen.*
> If I had studied more (but I didn't), I would have passed the exam.

> *Si **hubiéramos salido** antes, no **habríamos** llegado tarde.*
> If we had left earlier (but we didn't), we wouldn't have arrived late.

> *Si me **hubieran avisado**, **habría** traído más comida.*
> If they had warned me (but they didn't), I would have brought more food.

---

## Mixed Conditionals

Sometimes the condition and the result are from different time frames. These are called mixed conditionals.

### Past condition → present result

> *Si **hubiera** nacido en otra ciudad, **sería** diferente ahora.*
> If I had been born in another city (past), I would be different now (present).

### Present condition → past result

> *Si **fuera** más organizado, **no habría** perdido esa cita.*
> If I were more organized (present character), I wouldn't have missed that appointment (past result).

---

## Important Rule — Never "Si + Present Subjunctive"

One of the most common errors among learners is placing the present subjunctive after *si* in a conditional sentence. **This is not correct in standard Spanish.**

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Correct |
|----------|-----------|
| Si tengas tiempo... | Si tienes tiempo... (Type 1) |
| Si puedas venir... | Si pudieras venir... (Type 2) |

The si of conditional sentences follows its own tense rules — it never takes the present subjunctive.

---

## Quick Reference Chart

| Type | Condition | Result |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Real / Likely | si + present indicative | present / future / command |
| Hypothetical | si + past subjunctive | conditional |
| Contrary to past fact | si + past perfect subjunctive | conditional perfect |

---

## Common Conversation Patterns

> *Si yo **fuera** tú, **hablaría** con ella hoy.*
> If I were you, I'd talk to her today.

> *¿Y si **cambiáramos** de tema?*
> What if we changed the subject? (polite suggestion)

> *Si lo **hubiera** sabido antes, **no habría** venido.*
> If I had known sooner, I wouldn't have come.

---

## 🇲🇽 Cultural Note — Hypothetical Talk in Mexican Conversation

Hypothetical si-clauses are extremely common in casual Mexican conversation for making **indirect suggestions** and **polite proposals**. Instead of saying *Deberías hablar con tu jefe* (You should talk to your boss), a Mexican speaker might say:

> *Si **yo estuviera** en tu lugar, **hablaría** con el jefe.*
> If I were in your place, I'd talk to the boss.

This frames advice as personal perspective rather than instruction, making it culturally smoother. Learning to deploy Type 2 si-clauses naturally is one of the hallmarks of conversational fluency in Mexican Spanish.
