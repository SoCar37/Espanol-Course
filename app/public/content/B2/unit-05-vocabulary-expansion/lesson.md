# Unit 5: Vocabulary Expansion Strategies

At B2 level, raw memorization of word lists becomes increasingly inefficient. The most effective path to a larger vocabulary is learning *systems* — patterns that let you decode unfamiliar words, generate new ones, and retain what you learn. This unit gives you those systems.

## 1. Word Families

Spanish words cluster into families built from the same root. Learning one word unlocks several others.

| Root | Noun | Verb | Adjective | Adverb |
|------|------|------|-----------|--------|
| trabaj- | el trabajo | trabajar | trabajador/a | — |
| feliz- | la felicidad | — | feliz | felizmente |
| escrib- | la escritura / el escritor | escribir | escrito/a | — |
| conoc- | el conocimiento | conocer | conocido/a | — |

> If you know *trabajar* (to work), you can immediately decode *el trabajador* (the worker), *trabajoso* (laborious), and *el trabajo* (the work/job).

When you learn a new verb, make a habit of asking: what's the noun? the adjective? the agent noun (the person who does it)?

## 2. Prefixes and Suffixes

### Common prefixes

| Prefix | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| des- | un- / reversal | deshacer (to undo), descansar (to rest) |
| re- | again | releer (to reread), rehacer (to redo) |
| in-/im- | not / in- | imposible, incómodo, inútil |
| pre- | before | prever (to foresee), predecir |
| sobre- | over / super | sobrevivir (to survive), sobrecargar |
| sub- | under | subrayar (to underline), subestimar |

### Common suffixes

| Suffix | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| -ción / -sión | action/result (noun) | conversación, decisión |
| -dad / -tad | quality/state (noun) | libertad, velocidad |
| -mente | -ly (adverb) | rápidamente, claramente |
| -oso/a | full of (adjective) | cariñoso, peligroso |
| -ero/a | person who / place | panadero, carnicería |
| -ito/a | diminutive | cafecito, ratito |
| -azo/a | augmentative / blow | golpazo, carrazo |

> **-ito/a** is particularly important in Mexican Spanish — it's used constantly to soften requests, show affection, or minimize: *un momentito*, *ahorita*, *cafecito*. Learning this suffix unlocks a whole register of warmth.

## 3. Cognates and False Cognates

### True cognates — free vocabulary

Spanish and English share thousands of words from Latin and Greek roots. Recognizing patterns gives you words for free:

| English pattern | Spanish pattern | Examples |
|----------------|-----------------|---------|
| -tion | -ción | nation/nación, solution/solución |
| -ty | -dad | liberty/libertad, university/universidad |
| -ous | -oso | nervous/nervioso, famous/famoso |
| -al | -al | natural/natural, formal/formal |
| -ism | -ismo | capitalism/capitalismo |

### False cognates — common traps

| Spanish word | Looks like | Actual meaning |
|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| embarazada | embarrassed | pregnant |
| éxito | exit | success |
| actual | actual | current / present-day |
| realizar | to realize | to carry out / to achieve |
| recordar | to record | to remember |
| molestar | to molest | to bother / to annoy |
| sensible | sensible | sensitive |

> **"Estoy embarazada"** does NOT mean "I'm embarrassed" — it means "I'm pregnant." One of the most important false cognates to know.

## 4. Guessing from Context

When you encounter an unknown word, use these strategies before reaching for a dictionary:

1. **Part of speech** — Is it a noun, verb, adjective? The sentence structure tells you.
2. **Word family clues** — Does it share a root with a word you know?
3. **Prefix/suffix** — Can you decode any part of it?
4. **Context** — What would make logical sense here?
5. **Cognate check** — Does it resemble an English word?

> *"El gobierno implementó medidas para contrarrestar la inflación."*
> Even if you don't know *contrarrestar*, you can guess: *contra-* (against) + *restar* (related to resist/counter) + context (fighting inflation) = to counteract.

## 5. Collocations

Words have preferred partners — learning collocations (natural word combinations) sounds much more native than correct-but-unnatural word choices.

| Instead of... | Say... |
|---------------|--------|
| hacer un error | cometer un error |
| tomar una foto | sacar una foto (Mexico) |
| hacer una pregunta | hacer una pregunta ✓ |
| tener miedo | tener miedo ✓ |
| hacer daño | hacer daño ✓ / causar daño |

> In Mexico, *sacar una foto* is strongly preferred over *tomar una foto* — this is a collocation worth locking in.

## 6. Register Awareness

The same concept often has a formal word, a neutral word, and a colloquial word. Knowing all three lets you adapt:

| Formal | Neutral | Colloquial (Mexico) |
|--------|---------|---------------------|
| fallecer | morir | morirse, petatear |
| residencia | casa | casa, jale |
| vehículo | carro | carro, nave |
| dinero | dinero | lana, feria, varo |
| trabajo | trabajo | chamba, jale |
| comer | comer | echarse algo, jalar |

## 🇲🇽 Cultural Note: Mexican Diminutives as Social Glue

In Mexico, the diminutive suffix *-ito/a* does far more than make things small. It softens commands (*espérate* → *espérame tantito*), shows warmth (*¿un cafecito?*), signals humility (*tengo un problemita*), and even intensifies urgency (*ahorita* — which paradoxically can mean anything from "right now" to "sometime today"). Mastering when and how Mexicans use diminutives is one of the highest-yield investments in sounding genuinely Mexican rather than textbook-Spanish.
