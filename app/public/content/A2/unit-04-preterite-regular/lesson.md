# Preterite Tense — Regular Verbs

The **preterite** (el pretérito) is the main past tense in Spanish for completed actions. If something happened and it's done — you use the preterite.

## Preterite Endings for Regular Verbs

Regular verbs follow two sets of endings depending on their infinitive type:

### -AR Verbs (hablar → to speak)

| Pronoun | Ending | Example |
|---------|--------|---------|
| yo | -é | hablé |
| tú | -aste | hablaste |
| usted / él / ella | -ó | habló |
| nosotros | -amos | hablamos |
| ustedes / ellos | -aron | hablaron |

### -ER and -IR Verbs (comer → to eat / vivir → to live)

| Pronoun | -ER ending | -IR ending | Example (comer) |
|---------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| yo | -í | -í | comí |
| tú | -iste | -iste | comiste |
| usted / él / ella | -ió | -ió | comió |
| nosotros | -imos | -imos | comimos |
| ustedes / ellos | -ieron | -ieron | comieron |

> Notice: -ER and -IR verbs use **identical endings** in the preterite.

## Preterite in Action

> Ayer **hablé** con mi mamá por teléfono. — *Yesterday I spoke with my mom on the phone.*

> **Comiste** en la taquería, ¿verdad? — *You ate at the taquería, right?*

> El partido **terminó** a las diez de la noche. — *The game ended at ten at night.*

> **Vivimos** en Monterrey por cinco años. — *We lived in Monterrey for five years.*

> Los estudiantes **aprendieron** mucho este semestre. — *The students learned a lot this semester.*

## Key Time Expressions for the Preterite

These words signal that the preterite is the right choice:

| Spanish | English |
|---------|---------|
| ayer | yesterday |
| anteayer | the day before yesterday |
| la semana pasada | last week |
| el mes pasado | last month |
| el año pasado | last year |
| hace dos días | two days ago |
| esta mañana | this morning (completed) |
| el lunes (pasado) | (last) Monday |
| una vez | once |
| de repente | suddenly |

> **La semana pasada viajé** a la Ciudad de México. — *Last week I traveled to Mexico City.*

> **Hace tres días compré** un carro nuevo. — *Three days ago I bought a new car.*

## Watch Out: Spelling Changes in Yo Form

Some -AR verbs have spelling changes in the **yo** form only to preserve pronunciation:

| Verb | Yo preterite | Reason |
|------|-------------|--------|
| llegar | llegué | g → gu before e |
| buscar | busqué | c → qu before e |
| empezar | empecé | z → c before e |

> **Llegué** al aeropuerto a tiempo. — *I arrived at the airport on time.*

> **Busqué** mis llaves por todos lados. — *I looked for my keys everywhere.*

These only affect **yo** — all other forms are regular.

## Nosotros: Present vs. Preterite

Be careful — for -AR and -IR verbs, the **nosotros** form looks the same in the present and preterite:

> Hoy **hablamos** mucho. — *Today we talk a lot.* (present)
> Ayer **hablamos** mucho. — *Yesterday we talked a lot.* (preterite)

Context (and time words like *ayer*) tells you which tense it is.

## 🌮 Cultural Note: Mexican Storytelling Style

Mexicans are natural storytellers. When recounting past events, conversations often begin with **"Fíjate que..."** (So, listen to this... / You won't believe it...) — a phrase that signals a story is coming.

> — **Fíjate que** ayer fui al mercado y me encontré con tu vecino. — *So listen, yesterday I went to the market and ran into your neighbor.*

You'll also hear **"la neta"** (the truth / honestly) when someone wants to emphasize the truth of what they're saying about something that happened.

> — **La neta**, no entendí nada de lo que dijo. — *Honestly, I didn't understand anything he said.*
