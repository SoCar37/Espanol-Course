# Idiomatic Verbs

A family of Spanish verbs works backwards from English. Instead of "I like tacos," Spanish says "Tacos please me" — the thing liked becomes the subject, and the person liking becomes an indirect object. Master this pattern and a whole family of essential verbs opens up.

## The Gustar Pattern

**Gustar** (to please/be pleasing) is the model for all idiomatic verbs. The key insight: **the thing** is the subject; **the person** is expressed with an indirect object pronoun.

| Indirect Object Pronoun | Person |
|------------------------|--------|
| me | to me |
| te | to you |
| le | to him / her / you (formal) |
| nos | to us |
| les | to them / you all |

Because the **thing liked** is the subject, the verb is usually **third-person singular or plural**:

> Me **gusta** el taco. — *I like the taco.* (el taco = singular subject)
> Me **gustan** los tacos. — *I like tacos.* (los tacos = plural subject)
> ¿Te **gusta** vivir en México? — *Do you like living in Mexico?* (infinitive = singular)

For clarity or emphasis, add **a + person** before the pronoun:
> **A mí** me gusta el mole. — *I (personally) like mole.*
> **A ella** no le gustan los deportes. — *She doesn't like sports.*

## Common Idiomatic Verbs — All Follow the Same Pattern

### Encantar — to love (something)
> Me **encanta** la música mexicana. — *I love Mexican music.*
> Nos **encantan** las fiestas. — *We love parties.*

### Doler — to hurt / to ache
> Me **duele** la cabeza. — *My head hurts.* (lit: The head pains me)
> Le **duelen** las rodillas después de correr. — *Her knees hurt after running.*

### Faltar — to be missing / to need / to lack
> Me **faltan** cinco pesos. — *I'm five pesos short.*
> ¿Te **falta** algo? — *Are you missing anything? / Do you need anything?*
> Nos **falta** tiempo. — *We're running out of time / We're lacking time.*

### Quedar — to have left / to fit / to be located
> Me **quedan** dos días de vacaciones. — *I have two vacation days left.*
> ¿Cómo te **queda** la camisa? — *How does the shirt fit you?*
> ¿Dónde te **queda** la oficina? — *Where is your office (relative to you)?*

### Molestar — to bother / to annoy
> Me **molesta** el ruido. — *The noise bothers me.*
> ¿Te **molesta** si abro la ventana? — *Does it bother you if I open the window?*

### Parecer — to seem / to appear
> Me **parece** buena idea. — *It seems like a good idea to me.*
> ¿Qué te **parece**? — *What do you think? / How does it seem to you?*
> Me **parecen** caros esos precios. — *Those prices seem expensive to me.*

### Sobrar — to have left over / to have too much
> Me **sobra** dinero este mes. — *I have money left over this month.*
> Nos **sobró** comida después de la fiesta. — *We had leftover food after the party.*

### Interesar — to interest
> No me **interesa** la política. — *Politics doesn't interest me.*
> ¿Te **interesan** los idiomas? — *Are you interested in languages?*

## 🍜 Cultural Note: Food and Gustar in Mexico

Mexicans use idiomatic verbs constantly when talking about food — arguably the topic they most love to discuss. When trying new food, you'll hear:

> — ¿Te **gustó**? — *Did you like it?*
> — ¡Me **encantó**! — *I loved it!*
> — Está rico, pero me **cae** un poco pesado. — *It's good, but it sits a bit heavy on me.*

The expression **"caerle pesado"** (to sit heavy) or **"caerle bien"** (to agree with you) — referring to food — follows the same indirect object pattern as gustar. Food "falls" heavy or light on your stomach — very Mexican imagery.
