# Formal Writing & Email

Written Spanish — especially in professional or official contexts — has its own register, conventions, and etiquette. This unit prepares you to write clear, professional emails and letters in Mexican Spanish.

## Formal vs. Informal Register

Spanish has two registers you switch between depending on context:

| Feature | Informal | Formal |
|---------|----------|--------|
| Pronoun | tú | usted |
| Greeting | ¡Hola! / ¿Qué onda? | Estimado/a / Buenos días |
| Closing | Saludos / Besos | Atentamente / Quedo a sus órdenes |
| Vocabulary | casual, contractions | precise, full forms |

In professional Mexico, even colleagues who know each other well often write formally in official communications.

## Email Structure

A professional Mexican email follows this structure:

```
[Salutation]
[Opening line — state purpose]
[Body — details, requests]
[Closing statement]
[Sign-off]
[Name / Title / Contact]
```

## Salutations (Saludos)

| Salutation | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| Estimado/a [Nombre]: | Dear [Name]: — standard professional |
| Estimados señores: | Dear Sirs/Madam: — unknown recipient |
| Distinguido/a [cargo]: | Distinguished [title]: — very formal |
| A quien corresponda: | To whom it may concern: |
| Buenos días / tardes: | Good morning/afternoon: — semi-formal |

> **Estimada Lic. García:** — *Dear Ms. García:*
> **Estimado equipo:** — *Dear team:*

## Opening Lines

> Me dirijo a usted para... — *I am writing to you to...*
> Por medio de la presente... — *By means of this letter/email...*
> En respuesta a su correo del [fecha]... — *In response to your email of [date]...*
> Me permito informarle que... — *I take the liberty of informing you that...*
> Con gusto le comento que... — *I am pleased to let you know that...*

## Making Requests

> Le agradecería que... — *I would be grateful if you...*
> ¿Sería posible que...? — *Would it be possible for you to...?*
> Le solicito amablemente que... — *I kindly request that you...*
> Quedo pendiente de su respuesta. — *I await your response.*
> En espera de su respuesta... — *Awaiting your response...*

## Formal Transitions

> Asimismo, — *Likewise / Additionally,*
> Sin otro particular, — *With nothing further to add,*
> En relación con lo anterior, — *Regarding the above,*
> A manera de conclusión, — *By way of conclusion,*
> Cabe mencionar que... — *It is worth mentioning that...*

## Sign-offs (Despedidas)

| Sign-off | Meaning / Register |
|---------|-------------------|
| Atentamente, | Sincerely / Attentively — most common formal |
| Cordialmente, | Cordially — warm but professional |
| Quedo a sus órdenes, | I remain at your service — very formal |
| Quedo en espera, | I await (your response) |
| Saludos cordiales, | Kind regards — semi-formal |
| Un saludo, | Best regards — casual professional |

## A Complete Sample Email

> **Estimada Lic. Ramírez:**
>
> Me dirijo a usted para confirmar nuestra reunión del próximo jueves 20 de marzo a las 10:00 h en sus instalaciones.
>
> Asimismo, le solicito amablemente que me comparta el orden del día con anticipación para poder prepararme adecuadamente.
>
> Quedo a sus órdenes para cualquier aclaración.
>
> **Atentamente,**
> Juan Carlos Mendoza Torres
> Gerente de Proyectos

## 📝 Cultural Note: Mexican Professional Formality

Mexican professional culture places high value on titles and honorifics. Always use professional titles when writing:

> **Lic.** — Licenciado/a (university graduate — very common title in Mexico)
> **Ing.** — Ingeniero/a (engineer)
> **Dr./Dra.** — Doctor/a
> **Mtro./Mtra.** — Maestro/a (master's degree holder)
> **C.P.** — Contador Público (CPA / public accountant)

In Mexico, a "Licenciado/a" is anyone with a university degree — the title is used far more broadly than in English. Not using it when writing to someone who holds it can be seen as disrespectful.

Also note: Mexican emails often begin with a brief pleasantry before getting to the point — a direct "I need X" without any warm opening can come across as brusque.
