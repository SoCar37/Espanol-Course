# Passive Voice & Se Constructions

In English, passive voice is common: "The letter was written." Spanish has a true passive, but it prefers the elegant **se** construction — used everywhere from signs to news headlines to everyday conversation.

## The True Passive: Ser + Past Participle

Like English, Spanish can form a true passive with **ser** + past participle. The past participle agrees in gender and number with the subject.

> La carta **fue escrita** por Pablo. — *The letter was written by Pablo.*
> Los edificios **fueron construidos** en el siglo XIX. — *The buildings were built in the 19th century.*
> El proyecto **será presentado** mañana. — *The project will be presented tomorrow.*

This construction is mostly found in formal writing and journalism. In conversation, Mexicans strongly prefer the se construction.

## The Passive Se

The **se pasivo** is used when there is no stated agent (no "by someone"). It's the most natural way to express passive meaning in spoken Spanish:

> **Se venden** tamales aquí. — *Tamales are sold here.* (lit: Tamales sell themselves here)
> **Se habla** español. — *Spanish is spoken.*
> **Se construyó** el puente en 2010. — *The bridge was built in 2010.*

The verb agrees with the grammatical subject:
> **Se vende** casa. — *House for sale.* (singular)
> **Se venden** casas. — *Houses for sale.* (plural)

You'll see this everywhere in Mexico — on signs, menus, and advertisements:
> **Se renta** departamento. — *Apartment for rent.*
> **Se hacen** arreglos. — *Alterations done here.*
> **Se busca** cocinera. — *Cook wanted.*

## The Impersonal Se

The **se impersonal** is used for general statements about what "one" or "people" do. The verb is always third-person singular:

> **Se come** bien en Oaxaca. — *One eats well in Oaxaca. / The food is good in Oaxaca.*
> En México, **se cena** tarde. — *In Mexico, people eat dinner late.*
> **Se dice** que el mole tarda días en hacerse. — *They say mole takes days to make.*
> Aquí **no se fuma**. — *No smoking here.* (lit: One doesn't smoke here)
> **Se puede** entrar sin reservación. — *You can enter without a reservation.*

## Accidental or Unplanned Events with Se

A very useful and very Mexican construction: **se + indirect object pronoun + verb** to describe unplanned events — things that "happened to" someone rather than things they did on purpose:

> **Se me olvidó** el celular. — *I forgot my phone.* (lit: The phone forgot itself to me)
> **Se le cayó** el café. — *He dropped his coffee.* (lit: The coffee fell itself from him)
> **Se nos acabó** el dinero. — *We ran out of money.*
> **Se me rompió** la pantalla. — *My screen broke (on me).*
> **Se te fue** el camión. — *You missed the bus.*

This construction removes personal blame — the event happened to the person rather than being their fault. It's used constantly in Mexican Spanish.

## Summary: Three Types of Se

| Type | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| Se pasivo | Passive without agent | Se venden tamales. |
| Se impersonal | General "one/people" statements | Se come bien aquí. |
| Se accidental | Unplanned events (happened to someone) | Se me olvidó. |

## 🪧 Cultural Note: Signs All Over Mexico

Once you know the passive se, you'll read Mexican signs everywhere:

> **Se solicita mesero** — Waiter wanted
> **Se compra oro y plata** — We buy gold and silver
> **Se prohíbe el paso** — No entry (passing prohibited)
> **Se atiende con cita** — Appointments only
> **Se hacen copias** — Copies made here

These signs use the passive se because nobody specific is mentioned — just the action itself. Reading them becomes effortless once you recognize the pattern.
