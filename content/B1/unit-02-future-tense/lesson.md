# The Future Tense

Spanish has two main ways to talk about the future — one you already know, and one that opens up a whole new level of expression.

## Way 1: Ir a + Infinitive (You Already Know This)

The most common way to express future plans in everyday Mexican Spanish is **ir a + infinitive**. It's like English "going to":

> **Voy a** llamar a mi mamá esta noche. — *I'm going to call my mom tonight.*
> **Vamos a** comer tacos el domingo. — *We're going to eat tacos on Sunday.*
> ¿Qué **vas a** hacer este fin de semana? — *What are you going to do this weekend?*

This construction is preferred in informal speech throughout Mexico.

## Way 2: The Simple Future Tense

The simple future is formed by adding endings directly to the **infinitive** — no stem changes needed for regular verbs.

### Future Endings (same for ALL verbs)

| Pronoun | Ending |
|---------|--------|
| yo | -é |
| tú | -ás |
| usted / él / ella | -á |
| nosotros | -emos |
| ustedes / ellos | -án |

### Regular Examples

| Infinitive | Yo | Tú | Él/ella | Nosotros | Ellos |
|-----------|----|----|---------|----------|-------|
| hablar | hablaré | hablarás | hablará | hablaremos | hablarán |
| comer | comeré | comerás | comerá | comeremos | comerán |
| vivir | viviré | vivirás | vivirá | viviremos | vivirán |

> El año que viene **estudiaré** en México. — *Next year I will study in Mexico.*
> ¿Cuándo **volverás**? — *When will you return?*
> **Llegaremos** a las diez. — *We will arrive at ten.*

## Irregular Future Stems

Some common verbs use an irregular stem but still add the same endings:

| Verb | Irregular stem | Yo form |
|------|---------------|---------|
| tener | tendr- | tendré |
| venir | vendr- | vendré |
| poder | podr- | podré |
| saber | sabr- | sabré |
| hacer | har- | haré |
| decir | dir- | diré |
| salir | saldr- | saldré |
| poner | pondr- | pondré |
| querer | querr- | querré |
| haber | habr- | habrá |

> **Tendré** que estudiar más. — *I will have to study more.*
> ¿**Podrás** venir mañana? — *Will you be able to come tomorrow?*
> **Haré** todo lo posible. — *I will do everything possible.*
> **Saldremos** temprano. — *We will leave early.*

## When to Use Each

| Situation | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| Casual plans and intentions | ir a + infinitive |
| Formal writing, predictions, promises | simple future |
| Wondering / speculating about the present | simple future |

The simple future also expresses **probability or conjecture** about the present — a very useful function:

> ¿Dónde **estará** Juan? — *I wonder where Juan is.* (Where could Juan be?)
> **Tendrá** unos cuarenta años. — *He must be around forty.* (He's probably about forty.)
> **Será** la vecina. — *It's probably the neighbor.* (when you hear a knock)

## The Future of Probability

This use is very common in Mexican Spanish — speculating with the future rather than using "probably":

> — ¿Quién llama a esta hora? — *Who's calling at this hour?*
> — **Será** mi hermano. — *It's probably my brother.*

> — ¿Por qué no contestó? — *Why didn't he answer?*
> — **Estará** dormido. — *He's probably asleep.*

## 🔮 Cultural Note: Mexican Fatalism and the Future

Mexicans have a famously philosophical relationship with the future — captured in the expression:

> **"Al cabo que ni quería."** — *Whatever, I didn't even want it anyway.*

And the omnipresent **"ahorita"** — which can mean right now, soon, or some unspecified future time — reflects a cultural comfort with uncertainty about when things will happen. The simple future in Mexican Spanish often pairs with softening expressions:

> **Pues, lo haré...** ahorita. — *Well, I'll do it... eventually.*
> Si Dios quiere, **estaremos** allá. — *God willing, we'll be there.* (very common phrase)
