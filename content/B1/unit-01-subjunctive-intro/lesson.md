# Introduction to the Subjunctive

The subjunctive is the grammar feature that separates intermediate from advanced Spanish speakers. It's not a tense — it's a **mood**, and once you understand what it does, you'll start hearing it everywhere.

## What Is the Subjunctive?

Spanish has two main moods:
- **Indicative** — states facts and reality: *Sé que viene* — I know he's coming
- **Subjunctive** — expresses subjectivity: wishes, doubts, emotions, possibilities: *Espero que venga* — I hope he comes

The subjunctive almost always appears in a **subordinate clause** introduced by **que**, triggered by something in the main clause.

## Forming the Present Subjunctive

Start with the **yo form** of the present indicative, drop the -o, and add the **opposite vowel** endings:

### -AR verbs → use -E endings (hablar → hablo → habl-)

| Pronoun | Ending | Example |
|---------|--------|---------|
| yo | -e | hable |
| tú | -es | hables |
| usted / él / ella | -e | hable |
| nosotros | -emos | hablemos |
| ustedes / ellos | -en | hablen |

### -ER and -IR verbs → use -A endings (comer → como → com-)

| Pronoun | Ending | Example |
|---------|--------|---------|
| yo | -a | coma |
| tú | -as | comas |
| usted / él / ella | -a | coma |
| nosotros | -amos | comamos |
| ustedes / ellos | -an | coman |

**Key insight:** Because you build from the yo form, irregular yo forms carry into the subjunctive:
> tener → tengo → **tenga**, tengas, tenga...
> hacer → hago → **haga**, hagas, haga...
> salir → salgo → **salga**, salgas, salga...

## The Four Main Triggers

### 1. Wishes and desires
> Quiero que **vengas** conmigo. — *I want you to come with me.*
> Espero que todo **salga** bien. — *I hope everything turns out well.*
> Ojalá que **lleguen** a tiempo. — *Hopefully they arrive on time.*

### 2. Doubt and denial
> Dudo que **sea** verdad. — *I doubt it's true.*
> No creo que **tenga** razón. — *I don't think he's right.*

### 3. Emotions
> Me alegra que **estés** aquí. — *I'm glad you're here.*
> Es una lástima que no **puedas** venir. — *It's a shame you can't come.*

### 4. Impersonal expressions
> Es importante que **hables** con el médico. — *It's important that you speak with the doctor.*
> Es posible que **llueva** mañana. — *It's possible that it will rain tomorrow.*

## The Key Rule: Two Different Subjects

The subjunctive is needed when the **subject changes** between the main and subordinate clause:

> Yo **quiero** que tú **vengas**. — *I want you to come.* ✅ (two subjects → subjunctive)
> Yo **quiero venir**. — *I want to come.* ✅ (one subject → infinitive, no subjunctive)

## Fully Irregular Subjunctive Forms

| Verb | Subjunctive forms |
|------|------------------|
| ser | sea, seas, sea, seamos, sean |
| estar | esté, estés, esté, estemos, estén |
| ir | vaya, vayas, vaya, vayamos, vayan |
| haber | haya |
| saber | sepa |
| dar | dé |

> Espero que **sea** un buen día. — *I hope it's a good day.*
> Que te **vaya** bien. — *May things go well for you.* (very common Mexican farewell)

## 🇲🇽 Cultural Note: Ojalá

**Ojalá** is one of the most distinctively Mexican words — and it always triggers the subjunctive. It comes from the Arabic *inshallah* (God willing), brought to Spanish during the Moorish period, and carries a warmth and philosophical resignation that fits perfectly with Mexican culture.

> ¡**Ojalá** que **gane** el América! — *Hopefully América wins!*
> **Ojalá** que **llegue** el fin de semana pronto. — *I hope the weekend comes soon.*
> **Ojalá** y **puedas** venir a la fiesta. — *Hopefully you can make it to the party.*

You'll also hear the very common farewell:
> **Que te vaya bien.** — *May things go well for you.* — said as a warm goodbye all over Mexico.
