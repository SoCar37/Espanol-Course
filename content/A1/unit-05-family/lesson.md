# Family & People

Talking about family is one of the most natural topics in any language. In Mexico, family (*la familia*) is central to daily life and culture. This unit teaches you to name family members, use possessives, and describe people.

## Possessives

Possessives in Spanish agree with the thing possessed, not the owner:

| Spanish | English |
|---------|---------|
| mi | my |
| tu | your (informal) |
| su | his / her / your (formal) |
| nuestro/a | our |

> **Mi mamá** es doctora. *(My mom is a doctor.)*
> **Tu hermano** es simpático. *(Your brother is nice.)*
> **Su abuela** vive en Oaxaca. *(His/Her grandmother lives in Oaxaca.)*

Note: **mi** and **tu** don't add accents in possessive use. The accented **mí** means "me" and **tú** means "you" as a subject pronoun.

## Describing People

Use **ser** (to be) for permanent characteristics:

> Mi papá es **alto y delgado**. *(My dad is tall and thin.)*
> Mi hermana es **joven y simpática**. *(My sister is young and nice.)*

| Spanish | English |
|---------|---------|
| alto/a | tall |
| bajo/a | short |
| delgado/a | thin |
| gordo/a | overweight / fat |
| joven | young |
| mayor | older / elderly |
| simpático/a | nice / friendly |
| antipático/a | unfriendly |

## Sample Dialogue

> — ¿Tienes hermanos? *(Do you have siblings?)*
> — Sí, tengo dos hermanos y una hermana. *(Yes, I have two brothers and one sister.)*
> — ¿Cómo se llama tu mamá? *(What is your mom's name?)*
> — Se llama Rosa. *(Her name is Rosa.)*

## 🇲🇽 Cultural Note

In Mexico, family gatherings (*reuniones familiares*) are frequent and large. It's common to have three generations under one roof or living nearby. The term *familia* often extends beyond the nuclear family to include close friends considered "like family." Addressing elders as *usted* is a sign of respect even within families.
