# Preterite vs. Imperfect

Knowing each tense is only half the battle — the real skill is choosing between them in the same sentence or story. This is one of the most important distinctions in Spanish, and once it clicks, your storytelling transforms.

## The Core Contrast

Think of a past story like a movie:

- **Imperfect** = the background, the scenery, what was ongoing → *sets the stage*
- **Preterite** = the main events, the plot points → *moves the story forward*

> **Era** tarde y **llovía** fuerte cuando de repente **escuché** un ruido. — *It was late and it was raining hard when suddenly I heard a noise.*

Here, *era* and *llovía* (imperfect) paint the background. *Escuché* (preterite) is the event that actually happened.

## Pattern 1: Background + Main Event

The imperfect sets the scene; the preterite interrupts with something new.

> **Dormía** cuando sonó mi celular. — *I was sleeping when my phone rang.*

> **Caminaba** por el centro cuando **vi** a mi ex. — *I was walking downtown when I saw my ex.*

> **Esperábamos** el camión cuando **empezó** a llover. — *We were waiting for the bus when it started to rain.*

The ongoing background action (imperfect) gets interrupted by the new completed event (preterite).

## Pattern 2: Habitual Past vs. Specific Event

> De niño **comía** tacos todos los viernes. Un viernes, **comí** unos tacos que me cayeron muy mal. — *As a child I used to eat tacos every Friday. One Friday I ate some tacos that made me very sick.*

The first sentence describes the habit (imperfect). The second describes what happened on one specific occasion (preterite).

## Pattern 3: Verbs That Change Meaning

Some verbs shift meaning depending on which tense is used:

| Verb | Imperfect | Preterite |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| saber | knew (ongoing) | found out |
| conocer | was acquainted with | met (for the first time) |
| querer | wanted (ongoing) | tried to / refused to (with no) |
| poder | was able to (in general) | managed to / failed to (with no) |
| tener | had | received / got |

> **Conocía** a María desde la prepa. — *I knew María since high school.* (ongoing acquaintance)
> Ayer **conocí** a la novia de Carlos. — *Yesterday I met Carlos's girlfriend.* (first meeting)

> **Quería** ir, pero no **pude**. — *I wanted to go, but I couldn't (didn't manage to).*

## A Quick Decision Guide

Ask yourself: **Is this a completed, specific event? Or an ongoing state/habit?**

| Signal words | Likely tense |
|-------------|-------------|
| de repente, entonces, en ese momento | preterite |
| mientras, cuando (ongoing) | imperfect for background |
| siempre, a veces, todos los días | imperfect |
| ayer, el lunes, hace dos días | preterite |
| era, hacía, había | imperfect (scene-setting) |

## A Full Mini-Story

> **Eran** las diez de la noche. **Hacía** frío y las calles **estaban** vacías. Yo **caminaba** solo cuando de repente **escuché** pasos detrás de mí. Me **detuve**, **volteé**... y **era** solo mi vecino con su perro.

*It was ten at night. It was cold and the streets were empty. I was walking alone when suddenly I heard footsteps behind me. I stopped, turned around... and it was just my neighbor with his dog.*

Notice how imperfect (eran, hacía, estaban, caminaba) builds tension through background, and preterite (escuché, detuve, volteé) delivers the plot beats.

---

## 🎙️ Cultural Note: Mexican Oral Storytelling

In Mexico, the art of **contar cuentos** (telling stories) is a social institution. Family gatherings around meals are prime territory for long, dramatic stories full of both tenses working together.

Listen for the shift: when a Mexican storyteller says **"estaba yo..."** (I was...) with the imperfect, you know a stage is being set. When the tempo picks up and specific events start firing — **"y entonces llegó..."** (and then he arrived...) — the preterite signals the action is happening.
